Your reputation is your pipeline

Five-star Google reviews aren't a vanity metric. For a local cleaning business, they're the single most powerful growth lever you have — and most businesses are leaving them entirely to chance. Here's how to make them systematic.

C
Cadi
6 minute read · Reputation & growth

When someone in your area searches "cleaning company near me" or "domestic cleaner [your town]", what happens next determines whether they call you or a competitor. They see a list of businesses. They look at the star rating. They scan the number of reviews. They read two or three of them.

That entire process takes about forty seconds. And in those forty seconds, your reputation — built over months or years of actual work — either earns you the call or loses it. You don't get to be in the room. Your reviews speak for you.

This isn't a small thing. It's the top of your entire funnel. Everything else you do to market or grow your cleaning business runs through the credibility filter of your Google rating.

Why great cleaners have mediocre ratings

Here's the thing that frustrates most cleaning business owners: they do excellent work, their clients are genuinely happy, and yet their Google rating is stuck at 4.2 with fourteen reviews — while a competitor with visibly worse service has 4.8 stars and sixty reviews.

The difference almost never comes down to the quality of the cleaning. It comes down to the system for asking.

"Happy clients don't review you spontaneously. They mean to. They even say they will. But life gets in the way, the moment passes, and you stay at 4.2 while they stay quietly delighted with your service."

The uncomfortable truth is that leaving review collection to chance is a form of neglect. The cleaning business at 4.8 isn't doing better work. They're just asking every client, at the right time, in the right way — consistently.

The timing problem

Asking for a review too early, too late, or in the wrong way dramatically reduces the response rate. Most business owners either don't ask at all, ask awkwardly in person at the end of a job, or send a generic message days later when the moment has passed and the client has moved on mentally.

70%
Of customers will leave a review if asked — but only a fraction are ever asked. The review gap isn't a satisfaction problem. It's an asking problem.

The ideal moment is simple: shortly after the job is completed, while the client is still in the warm glow of a freshly cleaned home. Not three days later. Not a week later when they've forgotten the feeling. Right after — when the house smells clean and they're grateful.

That window is precise. And hitting it consistently, for every client, after every job, is only possible with a system.

What a systematic approach looks like

This is what Cadi's Review feature does. Once a job is marked complete, a review request goes out to the client automatically — timed perfectly, personalised, with a direct link to your Google profile. No chasing. No awkward in-person asks. No forgetting.

When a review comes in, Cadi drafts a response for you. Not a generic "thanks for your review!" — a thoughtful reply that references specific details and builds the impression of a business that genuinely cares. You approve it or edit it in seconds. It goes live. Your profile looks active, engaged, and professional.

The compound effect
Ten new five-star reviews don't just improve your average rating. They move you up in Google Maps results, increase the confidence of every future prospect who finds you, give you more positive sentiment for Google's local ranking algorithm, and create a body of social proof that no amount of advertising can replicate. Reviews compound. The businesses that build them systematically leave everyone else behind.

Handling the reviews that aren't five stars

A systematic review process also means you get the occasional negative review. This is actually good. A business with 200 reviews and a couple of threes looks more credible than a business with 14 reviews and all fives. People know that a perfect record means either very few clients or a filtered sample.

What matters when a negative review arrives is the response. A thoughtful, professional reply to a critical review does more for your reputation than the review itself damages. It shows future prospects that you take feedback seriously, you engage professionally, and you care about getting things right. A bad review, well handled, can genuinely improve your reputation.

→
Acknowledge — don't dismiss or argue with the experience they had
→
Apologise — for the outcome, even if you don't agree with all the detail
→
Offer resolution — take the conversation offline if needed
→
Keep it brief — long defensive replies look worse than short composed ones

Reviews as a growth flywheel

Here's the long-term picture. A cleaning business with 20 reviews at 4.3 and a business with 80 reviews at 4.7 are not equally visible in local search. They are not equally trusted by the prospect who finds them. The gap between them compounds over time — as more reviews come in, as the rating improves, as the Maps ranking rises.

The business at 80 reviews didn't get there because they had better clients. They got there because they asked every time, every client, every job. That's it. That's the whole strategy. The reviews are there — in the happy clients who haven't been asked yet. You just have to unlock them.

Your reputation is already being built. The question is whether you're building it intentionally — or leaving it to the random subset of clients who happened to review you without being asked.
