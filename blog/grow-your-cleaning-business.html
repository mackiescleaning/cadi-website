3 tactical ways to immensely grow your cleaning business

Not theory. Not "get more clients" or "post on social media." Three specific, actionable moves that cleaning business owners have used to grow significantly — and exactly why each one works.

C
Cadi
6 minute read · Cleaning business growth strategy

Most cleaning business growth advice is generic. Build a website. Get more reviews. Post consistently. Put a branded van on the road. These things aren't wrong — but they're vague, slow, and hard to act on specifically.

What follows are three tactical moves. Not broad principles. Specific things you can start doing this week, with a clear explanation of why each one works.

1. Raise your prices — across the board, right now

This is the move most cleaning business owners are avoiding. Not because they don't know they should, but because it feels risky. What if clients leave? What if the phone stops ringing?

Here's what the data actually shows: most cleaning businesses are undercharging by 15–25% relative to their local market and their actual costs. A price increase of £1–2 per hour applied across your existing client base is the highest-leverage financial move available to you — and the client reaction is almost always milder than you fear.

~5%
The typical churn rate from a well-communicated, justified price increase. You lose very few clients — and the ones you lose are usually your lowest-margin ones anyway.

The key word is communicated. Don't silently update an invoice. Write to your clients individually and explain why. Rising costs. NLW increases. Commitment to quality. Clients who value your service expect prices to increase; they just want to be told why and given notice. The ones who leave were already at risk. The ones who stay are now worth significantly more to your business.

The compounding effect is the part nobody talks about. If you have 40 clients and raise rates by £1.50/hr, you might earn an additional £3,000–£5,000 per year from the same work, the same clients, the same hours. No new marketing. No new team members. Just a decision you've been putting off.

How to do it
Write to each client at least 28 days before the increase takes effect. Name a specific date. Give a brief reason. Thank them for their continued trust. Keep it short. Most won't reply. A small number will say thanks for letting them know. Very few will leave. And when they do, your capacity opens up for higher-value replacements.

2. Convert one-off clients to recurring bookings

If your business is built on one-off jobs, you're starting from zero every month. There's no floor under your revenue, no predictability, and no compounding. The moment your acquisition slows — because the season changes, because you take a week off — your revenue follows it down.

Recurring clients change the maths completely. A business with 30 recurring clients at £150/month has £4,500 of locked-in revenue before it picks up the phone. The same business built on one-offs has £0 of certainty at the start of each month.

The move is simple: go through your one-off client list and make them an offer. For end-of-tenancy clients, offer aftercare — a monthly maintenance clean for tenants moving in. For one-off deep cleans, offer a regular maintenance clean at a modest discount for commitment. For seasonal exterior clients, offer an annual maintenance package covering multiple visits.

→
Identify your last 20 one-off clients. How many are genuinely one-off by nature? How many are one-off because you never offered them recurring?
→
Make them a recurring offer with a modest incentive. 10% off their regular price in exchange for a standing booking. Most people prefer predictability — they just need to be asked.
→
Track the conversion rate. If you're converting fewer than 30% of eligible one-off clients to recurring, the offer or the communication needs adjusting.

Every one-off client you convert to recurring is worth three times the revenue over twelve months — and costs nothing in additional acquisition. This is the highest-ROI sales move available to most cleaning businesses.

3. Win one commercial contract

Residential cleaning is the natural starting point. The sales cycle is short, the jobs are accessible, and word of mouth works well. But residential has a ceiling — both on individual job value and on how many clients one team can manage in a given area.

Commercial cleaning changes the model. A single commercial contract — an office, a school, a medical practice, a block of managed flats — can be worth £800–£2,500 per month. It typically involves predictable hours, clear specifications, and invoices that get paid without chasing. And once you have one, winning the second is significantly easier.

The first commercial contract is the hardest because you don't have commercial references. The way around this is to start close to home:

→
Businesses you already have relationships with. Your accountant, your solicitor, your insurance broker — all of them occupy premises that need cleaning. Ask. You already have a relationship; that's most of the sale.
→
Local businesses on your existing residential routes. If you're already in the area, you're cheaper to deploy and a known quantity. Knock on the door. Leave a quote. Follow up.
→
Property management companies. They manage dozens of properties and need reliable cleaning contractors for each. A relationship with one property manager can produce a steady stream of end-of-tenancy work — the starting point for a commercial arm.

One commercial contract at £1,200/month is worth £14,400 per year. It takes one team in a predictable rhythm. And it becomes the reference that unlocks the next one. This is how residential cleaning businesses grow into something larger.

"Most cleaning businesses that want to grow focus on acquisition. The fastest-growing ones focus on raising value from what they already have — better prices, recurring revenue, higher-ticket commercial work. The foundation comes first."
