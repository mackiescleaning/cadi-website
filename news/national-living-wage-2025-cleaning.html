National Living Wage rises to £12.21 in April 2025: what cleaning business owners need to recalculate

The National Living Wage increased to £12.21 per hour from 6 April 2025 — a 6.7% rise. For cleaning businesses with employees, the cost increase is real and specific. Here's how to calculate it exactly, and what it means for your pricing.

C
Cadi editorial
15 Apr 2025 · 4 min read

Every April, the National Living Wage rises and cleaning business owners absorb the cost, often without formally reviewing their pricing. This year's increase is larger than most — 6.7% for workers aged 21 and over — and the additional employer National Insurance on top of higher wages means the real cost increase to your business is steeper than the headline rate suggests.

This article gives you the precise numbers, a worked example for common staffing configurations, and a framework for deciding whether and how to pass costs through to clients.

The new rates from April 2025

The following rates took effect from 6 April 2025:

Age group Old rate (2024–25) New rate (2025–26) Increase
21 and over (NLW) £11.44/hr £12.21/hr +6.7%
18–20 £8.60/hr £10.18/hr +18.4%
Under 18 £6.40/hr £7.55/hr +18.0%
Apprentice £6.40/hr £7.55/hr +18.0%

The most significant change for cleaning businesses employing adults is the 6.7% increase to £12.21. Note that the age threshold for the National Living Wage (the top rate) was lowered from 23 to 21 in April 2024, so anyone aged 21 or 22 who was previously on the lower rate is now subject to the NLW.

The 18.4% rise for 18–20-year-olds is a deliberate policy choice to narrow the gap between youth rates and the adult NLW. If you employ younger workers, the impact on your payroll is proportionally larger than the headline figure suggests.

⚠ These are minimum rates, not target rates
Paying exactly the NLW is legal compliance, not competitive pay. In many areas of the UK, £12.21/hr for cleaning work will make it harder to retain experienced staff. If you want experienced, reliable cleaners, £13–£14/hr is closer to the going rate in most regions.

Calculating the real impact on a cleaning business

Abstract percentages are less useful than concrete numbers for your specific situation. Here is a worked example for a cleaning business employing two cleaners at 30 hours per week each:

Old weekly wage bill (2024–25 rate, two cleaners at 30 hrs):
2 × 30 × £11.44 = £686.40/week

New weekly wage bill (2025–26 rate):
2 × 30 × £12.21 = £732.60/week

Additional weekly wage cost: £46.20
Additional annual wage cost: £2,402.40

That is before employer National Insurance, which we address in the next section. The table below shows the picture across common staffing configurations:

Staffing Old annual wage cost New annual wage cost Annual increase
1 cleaner, 20 hrs/wk £11,897.60 £12,698.40 +£800.80
1 cleaner, 40 hrs/wk £23,795.20 £25,396.80 +£1,601.60
2 cleaners, 30 hrs/wk each £35,692.80 £38,095.20 +£2,402.40
3 cleaners, 25 hrs/wk each £44,616.00 £47,619.00 +£3,003.00

These figures are gross wage costs only — before employer NI, pension contributions, holiday pay and any employment costs.

Don't forget employer National Insurance

Employer National Insurance is charged at 13.8% on earnings above the secondary threshold, which is £9,100 per employee per year (£175/week) for 2025–26. When wages rise, the amount above the threshold increases, so your employer NI bill goes up too.

Using the same two-cleaner, 30-hours-per-week example:

Each cleaner earns: 30 hrs × 52 weeks × £12.21 = £19,047.60/year
Each cleaner's NI-liable earnings: £19,047.60 − £9,100 = £9,947.60
Employer NI per cleaner: £9,947.60 × 13.8% = £1,372.77/year
For two cleaners: £2,745.54/year in employer NI alone

Compare to the previous year (£11.44/hr):

Each cleaner earned: 30 × 52 × £11.44 = £17,846.40/year
NI-liable: £17,846.40 − £9,100 = £8,746.40
Employer NI per cleaner: £8,746.40 × 13.8% = £1,207.00/year
For two cleaners: £2,414.00/year

Additional employer NI from the wage rise alone: £331.54/year for two cleaners.

Add that to the £2,402.40 increase in gross wages and the total additional cost of employing two cleaners in 2025–26 compared to 2024–25 is approximately £2,733.94 per year — before holiday pay on the higher wage rate, which adds a further increment.

ℹ Employment Allowance may reduce your NI bill
If your employer NI liability was under £100,000 in the previous tax year, you are eligible for the Employment Allowance — £10,500 from April 2025 (up from £5,000). This offsets your employer NI liability and for small cleaning businesses with one or two employees, may eliminate the employer NI bill entirely. Check whether you're claiming it.

Should you raise your prices?

In most cases, yes. Labour is your largest cost and the NLW increase is a direct and unavoidable increase in that cost. The question is how to handle it with existing clients.

Consider the margin arithmetic. If you charge £18/hr for domestic cleaning and employ one cleaner at NLW, your gross labour cost alone is now £12.21/hr — that's before employer NI (roughly £1.07/hr on top), holiday pay accrual (roughly £1.20/hr at 28 days statutory entitlement), insurance, materials, mileage, admin, and software. Your fully-loaded cost per hour of labour delivered is likely £15–£16 or more. Against a £18/hr charge rate, that leaves very little room.

⚠ The £18/hr problem
£18/hr is a common domestic cleaning rate that has not kept pace with cost increases. After NLW, employer NI, holiday pay, insurance, consumables and unpaid admin time, many cleaning businesses running at £18/hr are netting less than minimum wage for their own time. The NLW rise makes this more urgent, not less.

Most clients understand that costs go up. The challenge is the conversation. A few practical principles:

  • Give notice. Tell clients in writing at least four weeks before any price change takes effect. A brief email explaining the NLW increase and how it affects your costs is honest and professional. Most clients receive similar notifications from every service business they use.
  • Be specific. "The National Living Wage increased by 6.7% in April, which increases my staffing costs by approximately £X per week. I'm increasing my rate from £Y to £Z from [date]" is more credible than vague references to costs going up.
  • Accept that some clients will leave. Clients who leave because of a legitimate cost-reflective increase were not viable clients at the old rate either. Retaining them at a loss is not a sound business strategy.
  • Commercial clients expect it. Most commercial contracts either have annual review clauses or clients who understand that NLW increases are passed through. If your commercial contracts don't have this provision, it's worth addressing at next renewal.

What's next for the NLW

The Low Pay Commission, which recommends NLW increases to the government, has been tasked with making progress toward the government's stated goal of the NLW reaching two-thirds of median earnings. In 2025–26, median hourly pay is estimated at around £18.40, putting two-thirds at approximately £12.27 — the NLW is now sitting at roughly that level.

However, the government's target is a living standard target, not a fixed figure. As median wages grow, two-thirds of median grows with them. The NLW is expected to continue rising in real terms in coming years, though the pace of increase may moderate now that the initial catch-up phase is largely complete.

Budget for further increases. The NLW has risen from £7.50 in 2017 to £12.21 in 2025 — a 63% increase in eight years. Cleaning businesses that have not passed through proportional price increases during that period have seen their margins eroded significantly. Set prices now that can absorb a further 3–5% annual NLW increase without requiring emergency client conversations each April.
